DGB Review Boxes

Working for Dr. Feier in reconstructing his website for Doctor Good Back was a fairly standard task; nothing in particular stuck out to me outside of a little nifty review box I decided to whip up to add to the charm of the website.

Breaking the process down, I planned to build two classes which I ended up naming client and cHide with client holding the image and cHide containing the hidden text. To get the text to successfully show up above the filtered client class I had to relunctantly use absolute positioning as seen below. It works pretty well with square-based bios that can be listed as panels, so feel free to copy pasta this for your project no credit needed.

You can play around with it here

  • html
  • css

html

<div class="col-sm-3 client">
     <div class="cHide">
          <p>insert bio here</p>
     </div>
     <img src="img/album/clients/c3.jpg" />
</div>

css

.cHide {
     position: absolute;
     z-index: 2;
     top: 50px;
     height: 300px;
     color: white;
     padding: 30px;
     opacity: 0;
     -webkit-transition: .2s;
     -webkit-transition-timing-function: ease;
     transition-timing-function: ease;
}

.cHide:hover {
     top: 30px;
     opacity: 1;
     -webkit-transition: .2s;
     -webkit-transition-timing-function: ease;
     transition-timing-function: ease;
}

.client {
     padding: 20px;
}

.client img {
     width: 100%;
     filter: brightness(100%);
     -webkit-filter: brightness(100%);
}

.cHide:hover + img {
     filter: brightness(20%);
     -webkit-filter: brightness(20%);
}

the Word

Last semester a friend asked me for help on one of his IT projects which involved developing a mobile app from scratch. The idea for the project was similar to groupon, so I wasn’t too sure of the reason why it would be different/useful. Regardless, I decided to complete this project in a sprint; rather than developing my own UI from scratch, I incorporated W3′s cards stylesheet which was surprisingly intuitive and easy to use. It was built with a mobile focus, and it shows brilliantly when applied.

More importantly than being able to complete this webapp within a couple of hours, I learned the value of the webapp for startups in a hurry. Often times it can take weeks to make two versions of the same app for Android and IOS, but webapps can amend those problems; obviously not as a permanent solution, but certainly as a way to get things kicking within literally a couple of hours.

ITZ-LLC

I was tasked to redevelop a website I had previously created over four years ago which was, at the time, coded entirely via notepad as a means to challenge myself when I was younger. To avoid compromising the company’s established design and premises, I opted to recycle many of the concepts previously used from the outdated stylesheets.

The branding direction of the new website was to be “modern and young” yet “professional.” Before launching myself headfirst into development, I sketched out several possible low-fidelity prototypes to show the CEO and built a font-preview webapp (CFont) to determine a more concrete example of the concept at hand. We decided to settle on Lato and a version of the website that borrowed concepts from other IT-related firms such as IBM; while the presentation of the website is relatively traditional, the accented colors give it the subtle young appearance desired.

This site was built off a bootstrap base with the occasional jquery script and edits to the existing stylesheet. A logo revision was also made during this process to convert it into a square icon as opposed to a elongated vertical format.

You can visit the full website at itz-llc.com

Save Cultural Athletics

Due to the sudden risk of many organizations including the kendo club (in which I am an instructor), I planned, developed, and led the SCA Campaign with help from the Martial Artist Council at Virginia Tech. The website itself was made within two days due to a rushed schedule and required intensive prototyping development with many changes being made on the hour based on third-party recommendations. Before any major design decisions were made, I scheduled several meetings with many of the organizations at risk to determine a core narrative for the campaign which would run parallel to not only the main website but also marketing efforts through social media and physical prints. These discussions also took into account the important underlying politics beneath the campaign to ensure no repercussions from unintentionally antagonizing third parties would occur.

Within several days after the release of the website, SCA garnered over five hundred likes as thanks to the support from the Virginia Tech community, three articles written by the Roanoke Times and the Collegiate Times, a featurette on Humans of Virginia Tech, a legislation in favor of the campaign through the Residence Hall Federation, and ultimately the overriding of the decision to charge organizations in the spring semester.

You can learn more about this project at the website itself at saveculturalathletics.com

My Storage

Approaching this project I was tasked to make a user interface for a startup company called “MyStorage” which was an ambitious idea to create a rental space sharing website between college students.

Working with the development team, I was cued in several times that the creators wanted something that made users feel secure in a friendly way. As a result, I recommended them to shape the project in a manner in which the users would feel a sense of ownership and community, hence the name “MyStorage.” I decided to go with a material design blue theme to make the user interface applicable via web and mobile (not to mention blue is a color that prompts the feeling of reliability/security i.e. IBM, BRINKS, etc). I opted to go with Roboto to match this theme, but looking back I feel as though I could have been a tad more bolder.

Client Font

During my many run ins with clients who were unable to easily identify what their favorite font was, I came up with Client Font or CFont for short. Rather than complicating things by sending them links to google fonts or typecast where they would have to pick on their own, I wanted a webapp that could simply display fonts I would recommend to them as a designer. The fonts can be changed relatively easily for my purposes depending on who I'm working for.

Give it a try here. Or if you're interested check out the details on github here.

Kendo Club Ranking WebApp (vtkm.rank)

The Kendo Club at Virginia Tech recently experienced a significant growth in intermediate members from 3 in 2012 to well over 30 in 2016. In response to this, as the head instructor of the club, I decided to develop a webapp that could dynamically keep track of all of the members.

vtkm.rank was based and inspired off of many official sports websites, particularly atpworldtour.com. However, unlike many of the websites I based my designs off of, the limitations of my back-end server's capabilities was nearly crippling. Since Virginia Tech's servers provided to organizations disallow the use of SQL, I was forced to reimagine a solution to the problem at hand. I decided to continue using XML documents to store player and match data, which combined with an off-server C++ application designed by Austin Garrett, could be semi-manually updated on a weekly basis.

The webapp now is currently in full use and provides insightful data publicy to all the members of the club including rankings, h2h, matches (with video search), and more.

Give it a look here!

Heaven Bound Cremation Services

When it comes to completing fast jobs as a web developer that offers expedited services, it’s alot easier to be able to reference old work to stitch something new rather than starting from scratch.

My client, Ms. Turner, was looking for a website to be completed and uploaded within a day as their previous host removed the old website from his/her server. We went over the details fairly quickly over phone and email, and were able to determine a color scheme/core narrative, tone, and so forth to move forwards with. After sending a few low fidelity prototypes, I was able to start working on building the website within the hour + setting it up on a godaddy server for her to manage. I used an old template I had made for common static websites (she didn’t require a CMS based one) that was built off of bootstrap and simply plugged and chugged.

In reflection moving forward, I think it’ll be easier to have more template sites on standby that I created on my own to save both my client’s and my own time/money. There’s so many website builders/templates/etc out there at the moment so it really makes you think what hiring a web developer is actually worth and it becomes a challenge to convince clients why your services are worth the price. As of now I differentiate myself mostly through my speed of service, but that too can be difficult to prove beyond the occasional online reviews I get for my work. Just some food for thought!

Orchestra Logo

Orchestra or "Orca" is a project I am currently working on as an intern in the NIS department at Virginia Tech. Basically, ORCA is an attempt to automate many network provisioning tasks at Virginia Tech- this means making software that simplifies work between many different departments as it globally encompasses all of NIS services.

Working with the UX intern team, we first researched colors that would be appropriate; in fact, we actually started by looking into commonly used palettes in actual orchestras for inspiration. From this, we made the decision to use redberry as our color of choice.



The actual design of the logo itself was proposed by my fellow intern Zoey Ryu who made a rough prototype inspired by the 4-beat pattern seen in orchestration. After a couple of iterations with the rest of the department, I made a vectorized high fidelity version with a gradient overlay as seen above!
